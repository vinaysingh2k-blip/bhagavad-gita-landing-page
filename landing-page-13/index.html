Four Parenting Lessons

Four Gita Principles for Raising a Child You Don't Control

Real parenting pressures, mapped to the chapters that address them directly.

🌱

Full Effort, No Guarantee

Chapter 01 · Gita 2.47

You have a right to your work, never its fruits. Your responsibility is the consistency and care you bring daily — not a guaranteed result you were never able to fully control.

🧘

Discipline Without Anger

Chapter 03 · Gita 2.62

Attachment gives rise to desire, and desire, when obstructed, gives rise to anger. Naming the thwarted expectation behind your frustration lets you discipline for your child's benefit, not to relieve your own.

🪁

Letting Them Struggle

Chapter 04 · Gita 2.3

Krishna taught and clarified — but left the actual choice, and the growth in making it, with Arjuna. Some of the best help looks like staying present without taking over.

🔁

Patience as a Practiced Skill

Chapter 06 · Gita 6.35

Through practice and detachment, the mind can be brought under control. Patience isn't a fixed trait — it's a capacity built session by session, like any other discipline.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this a translation of the Gita?

No. Gita for Grounded Parenting is an original, contemporary commentary inspired by the themes of the Bhagavad Gita. It is not a translation of the Gita and does not reproduce any copyrighted translation of the source text. Each chapter cites one Bhagavad Gita verse — transliteration and English meaning, referenced by chapter and verse — and builds original commentary, a practice, and reflection questions around it.

I'm not religious — is this really useful for parenting?

Yes. Krishna's core instruction to Arjuna — do your duty fully, without attachment to how it turns out — is one of the oldest descriptions of parenting available. This companion draws out that tension specifically: full effort, without the illusion of full control.

Why twelve chapters, and how were they chosen?

This companion is part of a ten-guide series — Ancient Wisdom for Everyday Life — each applying the Gita to one area of life. These twelve chapters draw on verses from across the Gita, chosen specifically for what they say about raising a child you love and don't fully control.

Who is this book written for?

Parents at any stage, with any parenting philosophy, who are trying, imperfectly, with a child they love and don't fully control. It doesn't prescribe a parenting technique — it offers a steadier posture to bring to whatever technique you're already using.
