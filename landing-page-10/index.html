Four Money Lessons

Four Gita-Inspired Principles for a Steadier Relationship With Money

Real, everyday money patterns, mapped to the chapters that take them apart directly.

🪷

The Fruit Was Never the Point

Chapter 01 · 2.47

You have a right to your work, never to its fruits. Money pursued as the entire reason for acting becomes a moving target — "enough" keeps redefining itself upward.

🚪

Greed Rarely Announces Itself

Chapter 03 · 16.21

Desire, anger, and greed are named as a threefold gate to self-destruction. Greed usually arrives dressed as reasonable planning or "just being smart" — recognizing it means checking the motive, not the justification.

📋

The List That Never Finishes

Chapter 05 · 16.13

The greedy mind's monologue — this I have, this I'll get next — never reaches a natural stopping point. Comparison behaves the same way; more just produces a new comparison to make.

🌊

Abundance as Already Full

Chapter 12 · 2.70

Like an ocean that rivers flow into without ever changing its fundamental state, peace belongs to the one desires flow into — not the one chasing desires outward.

"As rivers flow into the ocean, which remains ever full and unmoved, so one whom all desires enter finds peace — not the one who chases desires."

— Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 70
