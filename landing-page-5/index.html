Four Focus Lessons

Four Gita-Inspired Ideas for a Distracted Age

Real workday situations, mapped to the chapters that address them directly.

🎯

Name the One Task

Chapter 1 · Bhagavad Gita 1.1

Clarity comes before action, not after it. You don't fix scattered attention with more willpower — you fix it by first naming, plainly, what the one task in front of you actually is.

🧗

Effort Over Outcome

Chapter 2 · Bhagavad Gita 2.47

Finish your part of the transaction — the effort, the craft, the decision — and treat what happens next as a separate, later event that doesn't need your attention right now.

⏳

Begin Before You Feel Ready

Chapter 6 · Bhagavad Gita 2.3

Inaction is not actually available to us — the task left undone has already been "acted on," just badly, by neglect. Clarity often arrives during the act, not before it.

🪞

Lead Yourself First

Chapter 11 · Bhagavad Gita 6.5

You already know what to do — you've told a colleague this exact thing before. Self-leadership is simply applying that same patient reasoning to yourself.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this a translation of the Gita?

No. Gita for Productivity is an original, contemporary commentary inspired by the themes of the Bhagavad Gita. It is not a translation of the Gita and does not reproduce any copyrighted translation of the source text. Each chapter cites one specific, publicly known verse by chapter and verse number, then offers original commentary, a practical exercise, and reflection questions built for a distracted, modern workday.

I'm not religious — is this really useful for getting work done?

Yes. The Gita is, at its core, a dialogue about acting clearly under pressure. This companion draws out the focus and discipline principles specifically, citing the verse each idea is inspired by.

Why only 12 chapters and not a full translation?

This companion distills twelve ideas from the Gita most directly useful for focused, sustainable work — distraction, effort without attachment, steadiness, and devotion to purpose — into short, usable chapters, rather than reproducing the complete eighteen-chapter text.

Anyone whose work gets derailed by notifications, procrastination, or an inconsistent mind — professionals, students, freelancers, and founders who want a steadier way to focus.
